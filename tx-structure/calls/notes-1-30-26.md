## Framing the Problem

The previous discussion focused on clarifying the core problem we are trying to solve.

We begin with Istvan’s model, where vertices with identical addresses are unified. In the general case, the adversary maintains a probability distribution over possible partitions of the graph. The set of all partitions forms a lattice, where both meet and join operations are defined. These operations are commutative.

For our purposes, we do not need to explicitly compute this probability distribution. Instead, we assume a black-box adversary model that selects the highest-probability vertices and performs random walks over the graph.


## Role of Wallet Fingerprinting

Wallet fingerprinting (WF) provides clustering information that influences the sub transaction model. The adversary iteratively refines their partition as they learn new information. WF affects how sub-transaction structures are interpreted and therefore influences the refinement process.

## Graph Model

We maintain a bipartite graph structure:

* Coin vertices
* Transaction vertices

Edges connect coins to transactions. One idea discussed was assigning probability labels to undirected edges between coins, but this may introduce unnecessary complexity.

A partition over the vertices defines a user graph. The anonymity set corresponds to the stationary distribution of a random walk starting from a chosen origin.

## Successor to BlockSci

We are effectively building a successor to BlockSci. BlockSci performs full graph traversal with built-in clustering assumptions. Our goal is more general:

* Operate over partition algebra directly
* Explore interactions between wallet fingerprint-based clustering and intersection attacks
* Evaluate how these interactions impact Payjoin privacy

This analysis has not been done before and may reveal weaknesses in Payjoin/Coinjoin privacy.


We will simulate graph structures that resemble expected mainnet behavior and evaluate them using our analysis framework.

## Absorber Entropy Def

Absorber entropy is defined as the Shannon entropy of the probability distribution over absorbing states in an absorbing Markov chain.

Intersection attacks correspond to minimizing entropy within this framework.

In principle, any heuristic can be represented as a transition matrix:

* Start with an identity matrix
* Refine it as more information is incorporated

However, most heuristics are better understood as clustering operations that partition the graph rather than purely linear transformations. The sub-transaction model can incorporate these heuristics and acts as a generalization of multi-input heuristics for collaborative transactions.

Amounts alone are insufficient in the sub-transaction model; wallet fingerprint information must also be incorporated.

## Key Research Questions

### Intersection Attacks

An intersection attack begins at two or more origins that later spend into the same transaction. We then examine the resulting probability distributions from random walks.

Open questions:

* What is the formal definition of intersection attacks in this model?
* When does the model overestimate absorber entropy?

We want to identify graph structures that guarantee a minimum entropy level, ensuring anonymity sets do not decay exponentially over time.

If sub-transaction constraints are not limiting, the associated subset-sum problem becomes dense and underdetermined. In this case, the primary constraint on the absorber set becomes graph topology.

## Toward a More General Model

We aim to generalize beyond Istvan’s model by allowing additional edge formations. Even under repeated intersection attacks, we want guarantees that anonymity persists -- or just have a lower bound.

This motivates the study of directed expander graphs, where stationary distributions converge to uniformity.

## Differential Privacy Perspective

Differential privacy offers a useful analogy:

> A small change in input data should produce only a small change in output probability distributions.

In our setting:

* ε represents a privacy budget
* The key question becomes how quickly privacy decays
* Specifically, how much adversarial information is required to push the graph from an expander regime into a sparse one

## Intersection Attacks and Graph Degradation

We want to characterize:

* How absorber entropy changes as edges are removed
* How many edge removals are required before entropy significantly degrades

Goldfeder et al. provide useful intuition for grounding these ideas qualitatively within Istvan’s framework.
